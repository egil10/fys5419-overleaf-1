\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Single-Qubit Operations and Bell State Entanglement}
\label{subsec:single-qubit-bell}

Applying the Pauli operators defined in \cref{section:methods} confirms the expected behaviour.

\begin{flalign}
\sigma_x \ket{0} &= \ket{1} & \sigma_x \ket{1} &= \ket{0} && \text{bit-flip} \\
\sigma_y \ket{0} &= i\ket{1} & \sigma_y \ket{1} &= -i\ket{0} && \text{bit-flip + phase} \\
\sigma_z \ket{0} &= \ket{0} & \sigma_z \ket{1} &= -\ket{1} && \text{phase-flip}
\end{flalign}

The Hadamard gate (H) maps computational basis states to equal superpositions, $H\ket{0}=\ket{+}$ and $H\ket{1}=\ket{-}$. The phase gate introduces a relative phase of $i$ on $\ket{1}$ while leaving $\ket{0}$ unchanged. In our results, both gates act as expected. 

Starting from $\ket{00}$, the circuit $H\otimes\textbf{I}$ followed by CNOT produced \cref{h-otimes-I-cnot}, and with repeated measurement of $\ket{\Phi^+}$ with $50$ runs times $1000$ shots yielded the table seen in \cref{tab:measurement-probabilities}.
\begin{equation}\label{h-otimes-I-cnot}
\ket{00} 
\xrightarrow{H\otimes\textbf{I}} 
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00}+\ket{10}\right)
\xrightarrow{\text{CNOT}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{00}+\ket{11}\right)
=
\ket{\Phi^+}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Two-Qubit System and Interaction-Driven Entanglement}
\label{subsec:two-qubit-entanglement}

The two-qubit Hamiltonian with parameters $H_x=2.0,\space H_z=3.0,\space \epsilon_{00}=0.0,\space \epsilon_{10}=2.5,\space \epsilon_{01}=2.5,\space \epsilon_{11}=7.0$ is diagonalized over $\lambda\in[0,1]$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/part-d_eigenvalues.pdf}
    \caption{Eigenvalues}
    \label{fig:figures/part-d_eigenvalues.pdf}
\end{figure}

At $\lambda=0$ the eigenvalues are the non-interacting energies $0.0,\space 2.5,\space 6.5,\space 7.0$. As $\lambda$ increases, the $\sigma_x\otimes\sigma_x$ interaction shifts the diagonal elements, pushing $\ket{00}$ and $\ket{11}$ up while pulling $\ket{01}$ and $\ket{10}$ down. The $\sigma_x\otimes\sigma_x$ coupling creates off-diagonal mixing between $\ket{00}\leftrightarrow\ket{11}$ and $\ket{01}\leftrightarrow\ket{10}$ producing avoided crossings. the block structure of the Hamiltonian, two independent $2\times2$ blocks, are reflected in the pairwise behaviours of the energy levels.

The von Neumann entropy is computed for all four eigenstates, not just the ground state. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/part-d_entropy.pdf}
    \caption{Entropy}
    \label{fig:figures/part-d_entropy.pdf}
\end{figure}

Some key observations; (i) zero entropy, where at $\lambda=0$ all eigenstates are computational basis states (product states), so $\mathcal{S}=0$; (ii) monotonic increase, where as $\lambda$ grows the off-diagonal interaction mixes the basis states, generating entanglement and the entropy increases smoothly; (iii) sharp jump at the avoided crossing, near the avoided crossing region, the entropy shows a rapid increase, the ground state transitions from being dominated by a single basis state to being strongly entangled superposition, a small change in $\lambda$ produces a dramatic shift in quantum correlations, (iv) Hamiltonian-driven entanglement, the entanglement is not externally imposed, it emerges directly from the competition between the diagonal spacing and the off-diagonal coupling $H_x$, when the coupling becomes comparable to the energy gap, entanglement grows sharply. 

The non-interacting Hamiltonian $\mathcal{H}_0=\text{diag}\left(\epsilon_{00},\space \epsilon_{01},\space \epsilon_{10},\space \epsilon_{11}\right)$ is decomposed into four Pauli strings, as seen in \cref{four-pauli-string}, with $h_0=4.0,\space h_1=0.25,\space h_2=-2.75,\space h_3=-0.5$. The interaction adds $(h_3+\lambda\mathcal{H}_z),\space ZZ+\lambda\mathcal{H}_x,\space XX$, giving $5$ Pauli terms total.
\begin{align}\label{four-pauli-string}
    & \mathcal{H}_0=h_0 \\
    & II + h_1 \\
    & ZI+h_2 \\
    & IZ+h_3 \\
    & ZZ
\end{align}

The hardware-efficient ansatz consists of two $R_y$ rotation layers seperated by a CNOT entangler
\begin{equation}
    \ket{\psi(\theta)} = \left( R_y(\theta_3) \otimes R_y(\theta_4) \right) \cdot \text{CNOT} \cdot \left( R_y(\theta_1) \otimes R_y(\theta_2) \right) \ket{00}
\end{equation}

This gives $4$ variational parameters. The $R_y$ rotations span the real subspace of each qubit, and the CNOT provides the entanglement needed to represent correlated states. For a $4$-dimensional real Hilbert space, $4$ real parameters are sufficient. 

The VQE sweep over $\lambda\in[0,1]$ compares three approaches, in \cref{figures/part-e_vqe_comparison.pdf}, where all three curves, exact, VQE, and Qiskit VQE, are indistinguishable. With the precision, the max absolute error for manual VQE and Qiskit VQE are both $\approx 10^{-10}$. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/part-e_vqe_comparison.pdf}
    \caption{VQE Comparison}
    \label{figures/part-e_vqe_comparison.pdf}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/part-e_vqe_precision.pdf}
    \caption{VQE Precision}
    \label{figures/part-e_vqe_precision.pdf}
\end{figure}

The $R_y$-CNOT-$R_y$ circuit can represent any real two-qubit state. This means the VQE achieves exact results, the error is purely from the optimizer, not the ansatz. 

BFGS converges reliably from the zero-parameter initialization $\theta=(0,0,0,0)$. No random restarts were needed, suggesting a smooth optimization landscape for this system. 

The exact match between the manual implementation and Qiskit validates the VQE-code, both correctly handle the ansatz, Pauli measurements, and optimization loop.

The VQE implicitly captures the entanglement structure of the ground state, as $\lambda$ increases through the crossing region, the optimal CNOT angle changes to produce the appropriate entanglement, tracked by the entropy analysis from the first part.
